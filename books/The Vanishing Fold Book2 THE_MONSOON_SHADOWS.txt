CHAPTER ONE

The Twilight Estate had the architectural swagger of colonial power trying to rebrand itself as heritage luxury.

White columns. Wide verandas. A central courtyard built on what had probably been someone else's land. The kind of place where wealthy people paid premium rates to experience "authenticity" while air conditioning kept the monsoon at a comfortable distance.

Ishaan arrived by taxi on October 14th, the day after a child had vanished.

The property was in controlled chaos. Police vehicles in the driveway. Forensic teams photographing room 7. Parents being interviewed in separate locations to prevent testimony coordination.

Ishaan checked in as an investigator coordinating with the central database. His credentials were valid. His official status was vague enough that local police couldn't refuse him but clear enough that they didn't need to cooperate with him either.

He took a room on the ground floor, thirty meters from room 7.

From his window, he could see the sliding glass door that opened onto the garden. He could see the landscaping that had been disrupted by forensic examination. He could see the exact path that a three-year-old would have needed to take to exit the room undetected.

What he couldn't see was how a child in her sleep clothes had made that journey without being observed.

What he couldn't see was who had lifted her from the bed and carried her through that door.

What he couldn't see was how a functioning resort with staff and security and multiple occupied rooms could lose a child in the space between a staff check-in and a parental discovery.

CHAPTER TWO

The staff logbook was in the back office, accessible to anyone who worked at the property.

Ishaan read the entry dated October 10th:

"Chawla family (Rooms 7, 9) has requested dining table positioned within visual line of accommodation. Parents plan to leave children unattended during service hours. Table should be positioned for optimal sightline to room 7. Family will be in residence October 12. Accommodation noted."

The notation was professional. But what it actually was, was an advertisement.

Someone had written down, in administrative language, that a room would contain an unattended child. That the parents would be absent. That the room location made the extraction operationally easy.

Someone had made the decision to use the staff logbook as an operational blueprint.

Ishaan photographed the page.

The handwriting belonged to the shift manager. A woman named Priya Nair. She was in her fifties, fifteen-year tenure, no criminal history. She'd noted thousands of guest requests. She'd documented thousands of accommodations.

But this notation was different.

This notation was a message to someone who would read it. Someone who would understand what the notation meant. Someone who had permission to act on the information it contained.

CHAPTER THREE

The child's name was Ananya. She was three years old. She had brown hair, dark eyes, and a distinctive medical condition.

Heterochromia. One eye brown. One eye green.

The condition was statistically rare in Indian pediatric populations. The distinctive appearance made her remarkable. The distinctive appearance made her valuable.

Ishaan learned this from the medical file that her parents had submitted to the resort during check-in. The file documented her health conditions. Her dietary restrictions. Her medical history.

It also documented her distinguishing features.

It also, effectively, advertised her.

The witness—a man named Michael Smith, retired engineer, living in a coastal property adjacent to the resort—reported seeing a man carrying a barefoot child near the beach access path at approximately 22:00 hours on October 12th.

The child was wearing pink pajamas with a floral pattern.

The child had distinctive eyes—one eye clearly darker than the other.

The man carrying the child was moving with purpose, not with the protective grip of a parent. The man was moving efficiently, practically, the way merchandise was transported rather than the way children were held.

The man was walking away from the resort direction, toward the river.

And then he was gone.

CHAPTER FOUR

The CCTV system at The Twilight Estate was state-of-the-art. Multiple angles. Digital redundancy. Power backup for sustained operation.

The footage from October 12th showed exactly what it needed to show and nothing else.

The lobby camera documented the family's arrival. The child was visible briefly—even in low-resolution security footage, the distinctive eye coloration was apparent. Brown and green, impossible to miss.

The dining area camera showed the parents at their table. The staff moving in coordinated service patterns. At 15:12, Manager Rajesh Deshmukh visible in conversation with a man identified as Vikram Kapoor, Management Services Coordinator.

The conversation lasted ninety seconds. Kapoor's hand movements during the conversation were distinctive. A specific gesture pattern. The same pattern visible in other facility footage from October 7th. The same pattern visible in other facilities, in other cities, in other investigations.

The ground-floor corridor camera was empty during the relevant time period except for one staff member exiting room 7 at 15:05 with a key card.

The garden perimeter camera showed the garden at 15:18. A staff member carrying a laundry basket. No entry or exit from room 7's sliding glass door.

But between 15:02 and 15:10, the garden camera experienced a power disruption.

Eight minutes.

The exact amount of time needed for extraction.

The disruption was not documented in maintenance logs. The disruption was not recorded in the digital backup system. The disruption existed only as the gap in footage—as the absence of evidence.

Someone had access to the CCTV infrastructure. Someone had the knowledge to create a power disruption that would appear as mechanical failure rather than deliberate manipulation. Someone had the authority to ensure that no one questioned why the documentation of that disruption had vanished from official records.

CHAPTER FIVE

Vikram Kapoor was impossible to locate.

Officially, he was still employed by The Twilight Estate as Management Services Coordinator. His employment record showed no gaps, no relocations, no indications of instability or concern.

But when Ishaan tried to interview him, the man was never available. He was on administrative leave. He was attending a conference in Mumbai. He was conducting inventory audits in other facilities. He was always somewhere else, always one movement ahead of direct investigation.

But Ishaan found something better than Kapoor's presence.

He found Kapoor's phone records.

The satellite phone call logs showed a pattern of communication that suggested coordination across multiple geographic locations. Encrypted calls to a number that was registered privately. Calls placed at specific moments that aligned with operational events.

October 3rd at 22:14—a call lasting 47 seconds. The recipient number was a satellite phone, location unknown.

October 7th at 18:32—a call lasting 2 minutes, 13 seconds. Same recipient number. Encrypted transmission noted by the billing system.

October 11th at 14:45—a call lasting 6 minutes, 22 seconds. Same recipient number. Encrypted transmission.

October 12th at 07:33—a call lasting 1 minute, 8 seconds. Same recipient number. Encrypted transmission.

October 12th at 15:32—a call lasting 3 seconds. Same recipient number. Single message delivery, possibly text-based.

The pattern was too precise to be coincidence. The calls increased in frequency immediately preceding the child's disappearance. The final call occurred at 15:32—approximately two minutes after the parents reported the child missing.

Someone was confirming operational status.

Someone was verifying that extraction had been successful.

Someone was coordinating with Vikram Kapoor across a distance of approximately 1,400 kilometers.

CHAPTER SIX

The Cuddle Cat doll was found among the child's belongings.

It was handmade. Traditional Indian textile. Cloth stuffing. Embroidered features. The kind of toy that a parent gives to a child for comfort and companionship.

The doll was taken as evidence and submitted for forensic analysis.

The cadaver dog—a bloodhound named Zara, specialized in decomposition detection—exhibited strong alert response to the doll.

But the alert wasn't for recent human contact.

The alert was for decomposition compounds.

The doll had been in contact with human remains. Not recently. Weeks or months prior. The doll had absorbed the specific molecular signature of death.

Ishaan understood what this meant.

The doll had been used as a courier. It had been transported from one location to another, carrying with it the acoustic signature of a death that had occurred elsewhere. It had been placed in the child's possession deliberately. It had been designed to create forensic evidence of a connection that official investigation wasn't supposed to make.

And then the child's mother, authorized by police to maintain comfort items, had washed the doll.

The washing had destroyed the biological material. The trace evidence had been compromised. The forensic chain had been deliberately broken.

The system had facilitated the destruction of evidence.

CHAPTER SEVEN

The laptop belonged to a German national identified as "Christian B."

He was a suspect in prior crimes in Portugal. He was a transient with no fixed residence. He was the perfect patsy—the kind of person who could be blamed for child predation without requiring proof or coherent narrative.

But when Ishaan accessed his encrypted email account, what he found was something else entirely.

The emails were dated back months. Multiple drafts. Multiple recipients. References to locations and prices and "high-recognition subjects."

One specific email, dated June 2024:

"The latest acquisition was exceptional. The distinctive coloration made transport more complex—the recognizability factor required modified protocols. But the facility has prepared specialized chambers for high-recognition subjects. The clients are particularly interested in this specimen. The bidding is already substantial. I estimate procurement value will exceed standard rates by 40 percent. Little Paradise awaits."

The email referenced "distinctive coloration."

The email referenced "high-recognition subjects."

The email referenced "specialized chambers."

The email referenced "Little Paradise."

And Ishaan understood, in that moment, that the child hadn't vanished into random crime.

The child had been acquired.

The child had been processed.

The child had been transported to a location that someone had marked as "Little Paradise."

A location that was connected to the resort in Goa.

A location that was connected to the mountains in Himachal Pradesh where Meera Sharma had disappeared.

A location that existed as institutional absence—documented in encrypted emails but recorded nowhere in official systems.

CHAPTER EIGHT

The coordinates pointed to North Sikkim.

The satellite phone that had been receiving Vikram Kapoor's encrypted calls originated from a restricted military zone. Elevation approximately 4,200 meters. Administration by Border Roads Organisation personnel. A location that did not appear on standard maps.

A location that was administered by institutional authority.

A location that was isolated enough to be operational.

A location where people could be transported and processed and held without interference.

Ishaan booked a flight to Gangtok.

He understood, by this point, that his investigation had become his obsession. He understood that somewhere in these mountains, a child with distinctive eyes was being held in a location that existed outside official reality.

He understood that someone wanted him to find this child.

Or someone wanted him to believe that the motivation for finding the child was his own.

By the time the flight touched down in Sikkim, Ishaan had made another decision.

He would infiltrate the location that the satellite phone had indicated.

He would discover what infrastructure existed in that restricted zone.

He would learn what mechanisms had been designed to process human beings through the apparatus that someone had constructed.

And he would understand, finally, what had happened to his sister ten years ago.
