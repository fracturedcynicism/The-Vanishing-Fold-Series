CHAPTER ONE


The abandoned car was on a mountain road that shouldn't have existed.
The Maruti Suzuki was parked at a precise angle, as if the driver had pulled off the main road with deliberation rather than panic. The keys were on the driver's seat. The doors were locked. The vehicle was undamaged.
This was the aesthetic of voluntary departure.
This was the signature of forced extraction made to appear like choice.
Ishaan approached the vehicle at dusk. The forensic teams had already processed it. The search had been suspended due to weather deterioration. The investigation had moved into administrative holding—the specific bureaucratic limbo where cases went when they became inconvenient.
He listened to the vehicle's acoustic properties. The way the metal had cooled in the mountain air. The way the interior space held the residual sound of recent habitation. The way absence itself made noise when you knew how to listen for it.
Five men had been in this vehicle.
Five men had been extracted from this vehicle on October 20th, 2024.
And four of them would be found dead in a location approximately 1.8 kilometers away, in a bunker constructed for military personnel in a landscape so isolated that sound itself became a weapon.
CHAPTER TWO


Coach Arun Sharma had the breathing pattern of someone who had lost control of a situation and understood that his loss of control would be documented against him.
"They were good kids," he said. "Functionally excellent. The basketball court was where they felt capable. On the court, the intellectual disabilities meant nothing. They were athletes. They were competitors. They were part of a team."
Ishaan listened to the structure of the coach's words. The way he was building a narrative that emphasized normalcy. The way he was trying to establish that these weren't lost or confused individuals. These were people functioning at peak capability.
"The away game," Ishaan said. "They won?"
"78 to 76. Final minute. Ketan Malhotra—the youngest—made the game-winning assist. The team was celebrating in the rest house. They were in high spirits. They were talking about the tournament coming up. They were planning strategies. They were functional. They were cognizant. They were present."
Coach Sharma produced the game program.
The margins were filled with handwritten statistics. Scoring notes. Performance evaluations. Observations about player capacity and positioning.
Rajesh Kumar: 14 points, excellent defensive positioning.
Marcus Paul: 18 points, strong second half recovery.
Devesh Thapa: 12 points, consistent play despite insulin regulation issues.
Ravi Singh: 16 points, exceptional rebounding.
Ketan Malhotra: 18 points, game-winning assist in final moments.
The handwriting was Coach Sharma's. The tone was celebratory.
The team had functioned. The team had succeeded. The team had been present and aware and capable.
And then, somewhere between Siliguri and Gangtok, the team had vanished.
CHAPTER THREE


The bunker was designated BRO-NSK-7.
It was a prefabricated structure, approximately 3 meters by 4 meters, constructed as emergency shelter for border road maintenance personnel. The structure had been decommissioned approximately 18 months prior. The structure was not regularly maintained. The structure was officially abandoned.
But the structure was not empty.
Inside, Ishaan found four bodies in various states of decomposition. He found ration crates containing enough food to sustain human life for extended periods. He found candles. He found ceramic stoves. He found the infrastructure of survival.
And he found the evidence that survival had been deliberately prevented.
The propane heater was functional. The matches were present. The combustible books were present. The materials necessary for creating heat in a location where temperature was dropping below freezing were all available.
But no fire had been built.
The bodies showed evidence of death by hypothermia. Death by exposure. Death in a location where the mechanisms for survival had been provided but the psychological apparatus for using them had been destroyed.
The men had been terrified. The men had been convinced that light or smoke would reveal their location to an external force. The men had preferred death by freezing to the risk of discovery.
CHAPTER FOUR


The P38 can opener was military issue.
The tool was folding, portable, designed for field use. The model had been standard distribution for Indian armed personnel for decades. The specific weight was consistent. The design was mechanical and efficient.
Ishaan found it in proximity to the bodies.
The can opener showed evidence of extensive use. Scratches on the metal. Patina development indicating weeks of repeated operation.
Most of the C-ration cans in the bunker showed evidence of crude opening—the church key method. A slow, punishing process that left jagged puncture marks. But one specific can—a tin of corned beef dated 1998—had been opened using the P38. The opening was precise. The edges were rounded. The process had been efficient.
Someone with military training had been in the bunker.
Someone with military training had been systematically opening the rations.
Someone with military training had been attempting to manage survival for the others.
Ishaan examined the autopsy notes on Gary Matias.
The body showed evidence of repeated trauma. The skeletal frame indicated extended malnutrition. The cause of death was documented as "exposure to extreme cold."
But Gary had died first.
The others had remained in the bunker with his body for an extended period, living with the corpse, eating the rations that Gary had systematically opened, existing in a location where warmth was available but where the terror of external threat had superseded the mechanism for survival.
Gary had tried to save them.
Gary had failed.
Gary had been the first angel in a location designed to harvest angels.
CHAPTER FIVE


The gold watch had a missing crystal.
The watch face was rectangular. The gold had tested as 14 karats. The inscription on the rear case read: "For service excellence, 1978."
The watch belonged to none of the five men.
The watch was vintage. The watch was forty years old. The watch had been present in the bunker. The watch meant someone else had been in the bunker. Someone had remained in the location with the men. Someone had documented their decline through the mechanism of maintaining a timepiece that tracked days and hours and the specific moment when survival became impossible.
CHAPTER SIX


Joseph Shones was a local trekker with acute altitude sickness and fragmentary memory.
But when Ishaan played back the audio of his original statement and asked him to elaborate, Shones revealed details that had not been included in the official report.
"The breathing," Shones said. "The man in the truck—no, not the man driving. The man inside. The man observing. The man directing. His breathing was distinctive. It had a whistling quality. In and out. Like someone who had to think about respiration. Like the mechanism wasn't automatic. The whistling sound was on the inhalation. The sound was rhythmic. The sound was terrifying because it indicated presence. The presence indicated awareness. The presence indicated intention."
Ishaan asked: "You heard this sound?"
"Through the cabin window, partially open. The sound was audible. The sound was the most distinctive thing about that moment because it was human but not natural. It was the sound of someone who had learned to breathe in a specific way. The sound of someone who had engineered their own respiratory mechanism."
CHAPTER SEVEN


The Cuddle Cat doll was found among the bedding in the bunker.
The doll was handmade. Traditional Indian textile. Embroidered features. Cloth stuffing.
The doll was identical to the item discovered in the Goa case. The same materials. The same construction. The same design.
Ishaan understood what this meant.
The apparatus was leaving signatures.
The apparatus was documenting its own operation.
The apparatus was ensuring that anyone investigating thoroughly enough would recognize the pattern across geographic distance and temporal separation.
The apparatus wanted the investigator to understand the scope.
The apparatus was providing evidence that investigation was not only possible but was designed into the operational structure.
CHAPTER EIGHT


The Alan Martin account provided the missing piece.
Martin was a local driver who had been on the road on the morning of October 20th. He had passed a bridge near a dam and had observed an incident that nobody official had wanted to document.
The Maruti Suzuki was parked on the bridge. The five men were outside the vehicle. A large truck—dark red, wooden flatbed—was parked behind them. Men were exiting the truck. A large man—bulky, aggressive bearing—was approaching the smallest member of the group.
The small man appeared terrified.
The larger man appeared to be grabbing the smaller man's arm.
The Maruti was then moved away from the bridge, off the main road, into the direction of the mountain pass.
Ishaan understood what this meant.
The men hadn't gotten lost.
The men hadn't made navigational errors.
The men had been coerced away from the main road by actors in the red truck.
The extraction had been operational.
The disappearance had been forced.
CHAPTER NINE


By the time Ishaan reached the BRO bunker, the case had officially closed.
The five men were documented as having made poor decisions in a restricted zone. The four bodies had been recovered and returned to families for cremation. The fifth man—Ketan Malhotra—remained missing, documented as "presumed deceased, remains unrecovered due to environmental conditions and geographic inaccessibility."
Ishaan stood in the bunker and understood what had happened.
The same apparatus that had taken Meera in the mountains.
The same apparatus that had taken Ananya in the coastal resort.
The same apparatus that was coordinating across multiple states using satellite phones and encrypted calls and the systematic documentation of disappearance.
The same apparatus that was extracting people, processing them, and integrating survivors into the operational mechanism.
He understood that his investigation had now moved beyond individual cases and into systemic understanding.
He understood that the apparatus was institutional.
He understood that the apparatus would never be stopped through conventional investigation.
And he understood that somewhere in the Sunderbans—in a location called Little Paradise—the operational center existed. The place where people were processed. The place where survivors were offered integration. The place where Meera and Ananya and Ketan Malhotra would be maintained as functional components of the mechanism.
Ishaan booked transport to the Sunderbans.
He would infiltrate the facility.
He would discover the operational infrastructure.
He would confront the Fixer.
He would find his sister's remains.
And he would understand, finally, that justice was not available in systems where institutional authority had distributed itself across enough nodes that no single investigation could encompass the whole.
